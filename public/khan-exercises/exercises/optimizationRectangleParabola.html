Let f(x) = B-Ax^2. The graph of f and the horizontal axis bound a region. Consider rectangles in this region based on the horizontal axis, and with a vertex on the graph of f. Note that, by geometry, such a rectangle is necessarily symmetric with respect to the vertical axis.

What is the greatest area achieved by such a rectangle?

(2*B - 2*B/(3))*Math.sqrt(B/(3*A))

Let A(x) be the area of the rectangle with vertex (x,f(x)). Then A(x) = 2x \cdot ( B-Ax^2). Note that by symmetry, we need only consider x >0.

Simplifying, we obtain A'(x) = 2*Bx - 2*Ax^3

Differentiating, we obtain A'(x) = 2*B - 6*Ax^2

Setting this equal to zero, 2*B - 6*Ax^2 = 0

6*Ax^2 = 2*B

x^2 = \dfrac{2*B}{6*A}

x^2 = displayMode(B/(3*A))

x = \sqrt{displayMode(B/(3*A))}, because we are only considering positive values of x.

Since this is the only critical point of A for positive values of x, and we know that there must be some maximizing value of x because A is nonnegative on the region of interest, we can say with certainty that x = \sqrt{displayMode(B/(3*A))} is the location of the global maximum of A.

Thus the maximum area achieved by all such rectangles is A(\sqrt{displayMode(B/(3*A))}) = 2\cdot \sqrt{displayMode(B/(3*A))} \cdot (B - A\left(\sqrt{displayMode(B/(3*A))}\right)^2) = 2\cdot \sqrt{displayMode(B/(3*A))} \cdot (displayMode(2*B/3)) = displayMode(2*B - 2*B/3)\sqrt{displayMode(B/(3*A))}
