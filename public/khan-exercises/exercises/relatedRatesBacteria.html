A colony of bacterial are growing in a Petri dish. They form a circular disk, which appears to be growing at a rate of Rate square millimeters per hour.

When the disk is Radius millimeters, at what rate is the radius growing in mm/hour?

Rate/(2*Math.PI*Radius)

Let A be the area of the colony, and r be its radius.

Known Rate: \left. \dfrac{dA}{dt}\right|_{r = Radius} = Rate

Unknown Rate: \left.\dfrac{dA}{dt}\right|_{r = Radius}

Introduction: I will first write an equation relating A and r using geometry. I will then implicitly differentiate this equation with respect to time to obtain an equation relating the rates \dfrac{dA}{dt} and \dfrac{dr}{dt}. Finally I will use the information given in the problem to solve for \left.\dfrac{dA}{dt}\right|_{r = Radius}.

The area of a disk is \pi times the square of the radius. So A = \pi r^2

Differentiating with respect to time, we obtain \dfrac{dA}{dt} = \dfrac{d}{dt}\left( \pi r^2\right)

\dfrac{dA}{dt} = \dfrac{d}{dr}\left( \pi r^2\right) \cdot \dfrac{dr}{dt}, by the chain rule.

\dfrac{dA}{dt} = 2\pi r\cdot \dfrac{dr}{dt}

At the time of interest, r = Radius and \left. \dfrac{dA}{dt}\right|_{r = Radius} = Rate.

Thus Rate= 2 \cdot Radius\pi \cdot \left.\dfrac{dr}{dt}\right|_{r = Radius}

Therefore \left.\dfrac{dr}{dt}\right|_{r = Radius} = \dfrac{Rate}{ 2*Radius\pi}

In conclusion, the radius of the colony is increasing at a rate of \dfrac{Rate}{2*Radius\pi} mm/sec at the time that the radius is Radius.
