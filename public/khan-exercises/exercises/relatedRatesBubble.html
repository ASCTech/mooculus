In a feverish dream, you hatch a theory that to make the largest chewing gum bubble imaginable, you need to blow it up so that the radius is increasing at a constant rate of Rate millimeters per second.

As a function of the radius r, at what rate must you blow air into the bubble to achieve this in {\text{mm}}^3/\sec ?

4*Math.PI*Rate*r^2

Let V be the volume of the bubble, and r be the radius of the bubble.

Known rate: \dfrac{dr}{dt} = Rate
Unknown rate: \left.\dfrac{dV}{dt}\right|_{r}

Introduction: I will first relate V and r by using solid geometry. I will then differentiate this equation implicitly with respect to time to obtain a new equation relating the rates \dfrac{dV}{dt} and \dfrac{dr}{dt}. Finally I will use the rate information I have about \dfrac{dr}{dt} to figure out a formula for \dfrac{dV}{dt}.

Since the bubble is a sphere, we have V = \dfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3

Differentiating implicitly with respect to time we obtain \dfrac{dV}{dt} = \dfrac{d}{dt}\dfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3

\dfrac{dV}{dt} = \dfrac{d}{dr}\left( \dfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3\right) \cdot \dfrac{dr}{dt}, by the chain rule.

\dfrac{dV}{dt} = 4\pi r^2\dfrac{dr}{dt}

But it is our heartfelt desire that \dfrac{dr}{dt} = Rate, so
\dfrac{dV}{dt} = 4\cdot Rate\pi r^2

In conclusion, to keep the radius increasing at a constant rate of Rate \text{mm}/\sec you have to increase the volume at the variable rate of \dfrac{dV}{dt} =4*Rate\pi r^2 \text{mm}^3/\sec
