Two row boats start at the same location, and start traveling apart along straight lines which meet at an angle of \dfrac{\pi}{3}. Boat A is traveling at a rate of A miles per hour directly east, and boat B is traveling at a rate of B miles per hour going both north and east.

How fast is the distance between the rowboats increasing T hours into the journey?

(A*A*T+B*B*T - A*B*T)/Math.sqrt(A*A*T*T+B*B*T*T-A*B*T*T)

Let x be the distance of boat A from the starting position, y be the distance of boat B from the starting position, and r be the distance between the two boats.

Known rates: \dfrac{dx}{dt} = A
\hphantom{Known rates: } \dfrac{dy}{dt} = B
Unknown rate: \left.\dfrac{dr}{dt}\right|_{t= T}

Introduction: I will first relate x, y, and r using trigonometry. I will then differentiate this equation implicitly with respect to time to obtain a new equation relating the rates \dfrac{dx}{dt}, \dfrac{dy}{dt}, and \dfrac{dr}{dt}. Finally I will use the rate information I have about \dfrac{dx}{dt} and \dfrac{dy}{dt} to to figure the rate I am interested in.

By the law of cosines we have r^2 = x^2+y^2-2xy\cos(\dfrac{\pi}{3}). Since \cos(\dfrac{\pi}{3}) = \dfrac{1}{2} this simplifies down to r^2 = x^2+y^2-xy

Differentiating implicitly with respect to time we obtain \dfrac{d}{dt} r^2 = \dfrac{d}{dt} \left( x^2+y^2-xy\right)

\dfrac{d}{dr}\left( r^2\right) \cdot \dfrac{dr}{dt}= \dfrac{d}{dx}\left( x^2\right) \cdot \dfrac{dx}{dt} + \dfrac{d}{dy}\left(y^2\right)\dfrac{dy}{dt} - \dfrac{d}{dt}\left(xy\right), by the chain rule.

2r\dfrac{dr}{dt}= 2x \dfrac{dx}{dt} + 2y\dfrac{dy}{dt} - \left(x\dfrac{dy}{dt} + y\dfrac{dx}{dt}\right), by the product rule.

At the instant we are interested in, x = T \cdot A = A*T, y = T \cdot B = B*T, so using the relation between the three variables we know c = \sqrt{A*T^2+B*T^2 - A*T \cdot B*T} = \sqrt{A*A*T*T+B*B*T*T-A*B*T*T}. Also \dfrac{dx}{dt} = A and \dfrac{dy}{dt} = B. Thus
2\sqrt{A*A*T*T+B*B*T*T-A*B*T*T}\dfrac{dr}{dt} = 2\cdotA*T\cdot A + 2\cdotB*T\cdot B - \left(A*T \cdot B + B*T \cdot A\right)
So \dfrac{dr}{dt} = \dfrac{A*A*T+B*B*T - A*B*T}{\sqrt{A*A*T*T+B*B*T*T-A*B*T*T}} (Do you understand geometrically why this rate has to be constant? Could you have used this to simplify your work?)

In conclusion, the distance between the two row boats is increasing at a constant rate of \dfrac{A*A*T+B*B*T - A*B*T}{\sqrt{A*A*T*T+B*B*T*T-A*B*T*T}} \dfrac{miles}{hour}
