Suppose that \displaystyle\int_{L}^{U} f(x) dx = intVAL and f(U) = fVAL.

Approximate \displaystyle\int_{L}^{U+h} f(x) dx as well as you can given this information.

ANSWER

Here is a generic picture of the situation (signs might not be correct, but the basic idea is):

graphInit({ range: [[-1,4],[-2,16]], scale: [80,20], tickStep: 1, gridOpacity: 0.00005, axisOpacity: 0.5, tickOpacity: 0.00004, }); plot(function( x ) { return ( f(x)); }, [-12, 12], { stroke: RED, strokeWidth: 2 }); line([1,0],[1,f(1)]); label([1,-.3],L) ; label([2.8,-.3],U); label([3,-.3],U+h); label([2,f(2)/2],intVAL) label([3.2,f(3)/2],fVAL) line([2.8,0],[2.8,f(2.8)]); line([2.8,f(2.8)],[3,f(2.8)]); line([3,f(2.8)],[3,0]);

So the little bit of extra area we get is approximately the area of the rectangle, or in symbols, \displaystyle\int_{U}^{U+h} f(x)dx \approx h \cdot fVAL = h*fVAL

Thus \displaystyle\int_{L}^{U+h} f(x) dx \approx intVAL + h*fVAL = ANSWER

Note: Rephrasing this, we are really saying that the derivative of the function \displaystyle\int_{L}^{x} f(t)dt at x= U is about f(U). This is the essence of the fundamental theorem of calculus. Meditate on this.
