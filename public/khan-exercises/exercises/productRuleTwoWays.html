Suppose that:

f(X) = Y1
f'(X) = M1
g(X) = Y2
g'(X) = M2

There are two natural ways to approximate f(X+h)\cdot g(X+h):

Method 1: First approximate f(X+h) and g(X+h) using their linearizations at x = X, and then multiply these two approximations together.

Method 2: Compute \left.\dfrac{d}{dx}f(x)\cdot g(x)\right\vert_{x=X} and use this to to approximate f(X+h)\cdot g(X+h) directly using the linearization of the function f(x) \cdot g(x) at x = X

Approximate f(X+h)\cdot g(X+h) using each of the methods above. Contemplate why they both give good approximations. What is the difference between the two approximations? Can you see how the comparison gives a good reason to believe the product rule?

Method 1 approximation: roundTo(2*place,(Y1+M1*h)*(Y2+M2*h))
Method 2 approximation: roundTo(2*place,(Y1+M1*h)*(Y2+M2*h) - M1*M2*h*h)

Lets do Method 1 first:

f(X+h) = f(X + h)

\hphantom{f(X+h)}\approx f(X) + f'(X)\cdot (h)

\hphantom{f(X+h)}= Y1+M1 \cdot (h)

\hphantom{f(X+h)}= Y1 + M1*h

g(X+h) = g(X + h)

\hphantom{g(X+h)}\approx g(X) + g'(X)\cdot (h)

\hphantom{f(X+h)}= Y2+M2 \cdot (h)

\hphantom{f(X+h)}= Y2 + M2*h

so

f(X+h) \cdot g(X+h) \approx (Y1 + M1*h) \cdot (Y2 + M2*h) = roundTo(2*place,(Y1+M1*h)*(Y2+M2*h))

Now lets try Method 2:

Just to have a symbol for the product function around, lets define p by p(x) = f(x) \cdot g(x).

Then method 2 is really asking us to approximate p(X+h) by using the linearization of p at x = X.

By the product rule, p'(x) = f(x)g'(x)+f'(x)g(x), so p'(X) = (Y1)(M2) + (M1)(Y2) = Y1*M2+M1*Y2

p(X) = f(X) \cdot g(X) = (Y1)(Y2) = Y1*Y2

So using the linear approximation, we can say

p(X+h) = p(X+h)

\hphantom{p(X+h)} \approx p(X)+p'(X) \cdot (h)

\hphantom{p(X+h)} = Y1*Y2+Y1*M2+M1*Y2 \cdot (h)

\hphantom{p(X+h)} = Y1*Y2+(Y1*M2+M1*Y2)*h

Therefore, method 2 gives us the approximation f(X+h) \cdot g(X+h) \approx Y1*Y2+(Y1*M2+M1*Y2)*h
