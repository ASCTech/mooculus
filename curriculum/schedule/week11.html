What is summation notation?

Sigma notation is a fancy way to write down a sum, by  using a big $$\displaystyle\sum$$ and writing down a pattern for each term. For instance, if I want to add $$10 + 12 + 14 + 16 + 18 + 20$$, I could write

$$\displaystyle\sum_{n=5}^{10} \left( 2n \right).$$
You can  practice summation notation on mooculus.

Sometimes you can evaluate sums in sneaky ways. For instance,  the sum of the first $$k$$ whole numbers, called a triangular number, has a particularly nice form, namely

$$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{k} n = 1 + 2 + \cdots + k = \displaystyle\frac{k \cdot (k+1)}{2}.$$
Other nice patterns include the fact that  the sum of the first $$k$$ odd numbers is a perfect square, which can be seen geometrically. There's a  formula for the sum of squares, namely
$$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{k} n^2 = 1^2 + 2^2 + 3^2 + \cdots + k^2 = \displaystyle\frac{k \cdot (k+1) \cdot (2k+1)}{6},$$
and the truly remarkable  theorem of Nicomachus which states
$$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{k} n^3 = 1^3 + 2^3 + 3^3 + \cdots + k^3 = \left(\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^k n \right)^2.$$
You can  practice these “power sum” formulas on mooculus.

Messing around with sums is fun, but there's a question as to why we're bothering. These sum formulas will end up being helpful when we work on “Riemann sums.”

What is area?

In our heart, we've all got an idea as to what “area” means, and it's not my place to destroy your intuition! Nevertheless, let's ask the question:  what is area? If one region can be cut up into pieces and rearranged to form another region, then those two regions must “have the same area” but what about the converse? If two regions have the same area, can I demonstrate that fact by cutting the one up into pieces and rearranging?

The trouble is things like circles, what with their curved boundaries: a circle of radius $$1$$ has area $$\pi$$, but so does a $$1$$-by-$$\pi$$ rectangle, and yet, I can't slice that rectangle up to get the circle. And yet, I can chop up my rectangle into little pieces which can be rearranged to be as close to the circle as I want. It's a limit! Thinking about area leads us to limits! And not just us, but even Archimedes was lead to this sort of thinking two millenia ago.

So how do we make this precise

Integrals are introduced on  Page 190 of the Textbook.

We'll  approximate areas by forming Riemann sums; if we want to approximate the area under the graph $$y = f(x)$$ and between $$x = a$$ and $$x = b$$, I'll first partition the interval $$[a,b]$$ into $$n$$ smaller pieces $$[x_0,x_1]$$, $$[x_1,x_2]$$, $$[x_2,x_3]$$, and so forth; then I'll pick a point $${x_i}^\star$$ in the interval $$[x_{i-1},x_i]$$ where I'll sample the function. My approximation to the area is then

$$\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n f({x_i}^\star) \, \left( x_{i} - x_{i-1} \right).$$
The limit of such things (over finer and finer partitions) is the integral, which  we denote $$\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x) \, dx$$.

I invite you to  gain some intuition for integration on mooculus.

Can we compute any of these integrals?

Somewhat surprisingly, we can  integrate the function $$f(x) = x^2$$ by hand. For instance, $$\displaystyle\int_0^1 x^2 \, dx = 1/3$$, which is remarkable, since we are computing the area of a curved region exactly. We can also  compute $$\displaystyle\int_1^2 x^3 \,dx$$. These calculations end up invoking our previous work on summation formulas.

You can do some of these  integral calculations yourself on mooculus.

Can we understand anything conceptually about integrals?

One thing to emphasize is that integrals compute “signed area.” Area, like length, cannot be negative, but if you integrate a negative function, you should expect a negative answer.

Another way to understand integrals is to fit specific integrals together into a family of integrals; less metaphorically, I am suggesting we study the  accumulation function given by $$A(x) = \displaystyle\int_a^x f(t) \, dt$$. By thinking about when $$A$$ is increasing or decreasing, we get our first glimpse into the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus coming in Week 12. You can play around with  accumulation functions on mooculus.

And the integral  satisfies a few “linearity” properties, like $$\displaystyle\int_a^b \left( f(x) + g(x) \right) \, dx = \displaystyle\int_a^b f(x) \, dx + \displaystyle\int_a^b g(x) \, dx$$.

Can we compute any other integrals?

There is a trick that can be exploited to evaluate some integrals; if you are  integrating an odd function across the origin. The trick is that, say, $$\displaystyle\int_{-1}^0 \sin x \, dx = - \displaystyle\int_0^1 \sin x \, dx$$, and so $$\displaystyle\int_{-1}^{1} \sin x \, dx = 0$$, which we have deduced without doing any summation calculations.
